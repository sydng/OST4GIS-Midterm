Philadelphia's Litter:
A Big Problem for the City

One issue that Philadelphia has yet to overcome is the
garbage that litters its streets. This problem has caught the attention of Mayor Kenney, who has pledged to make the
city 90% waste and liter free by 2035. In preparation of
this goal, a litter index of Philadelphia has been created.

To better understand how this goal will be met, this
application aims to answer the following questions:

  • Where in Philadelphia are the highest areas of litter?
  • On Philadelphia's major arterial streets, how does litter
    vary?
  • Are commercial corridors more likely to be near high
    litter areas?

On this page, you can filter data by street type and click on
the street segements to determine the litter index.

You'll notice that the litter index varies even on streets of
the same type.

The following pages will explore how litter changes along Philadelphia's streets.

Streets with A High Litter Index

A streets' litter index can range between 1 and 4, with 1
being the lowest block score and 4 being the highest.
These streets have a litter index of greater than 3.

Streets with a high litter index are pretty well dispersed throughout the city, which shows that the litter problem is
not any one neighborhood's issue. However, there does
appear to be a large concentration of streets a high litter
index in North Central Philadelphia.

Major Arterial Streets with A High Litter Index

There are few major arterials in Philadelphia with a high
litter index. An arterial road is a high capacity road, which
most likely has higher speed limits. Therefore, the few
number of major aterial roads with high litter indexes could
be attributed to less pedestrian traffic.

Collector Streets with A High Litter Index

In comparison, there are many more collector streets that
have a high litter index. This could be the result of two
things:

  1. There are more collector streets, and therefore, by just number alone there are more chances for collector
    streets to have litter
  2. Collector streets are used to access residential areas.
    Lack of residential garbage collection - i.e. people leaving garbage bags on the sidewalk until pick up - may be contributing to the high litter index along these streets.

Are more Commercial Corridors near High
or Low Litter Indexes?

Interestingly, the major arterials and collector streets with a high litter index, occur in areas where commercial corridors
are more dispersed. Noteably in North and West
Philadelphia. This indicates that the concentration of people
on commercial corridors may not be the only factor that contributes to litter in those areas. There may be an
underlying neighborhood effect.
